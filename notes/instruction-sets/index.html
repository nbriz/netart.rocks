In another class we discussed the 1970 art and technology exhibition "Software". Among the many connections curator Jack Burnham was making between the conceptual art movment and the new information technologies of the time was the separation of "ideas" form "objects" in art, in the exhibition catalogue he writes "our bodies are hardware, our behavior software", which echoes Sol Lewitt's famous quote...

In conceptual art the idea or concept is the most important aspect of the work. When an artist uses a conceptual form of art, it means that all of the planning and decisions are made beforehand and the execution is a perfunctory affair. The idea becomes a machine that makes the art.

Sol Lewitt

The first two images are examples of instructions Sol Lewitt would send to a museum or gallery, the last image is an example of the "executed" painting (created by the gallery assistants following Lewitt's instructions)

Some artists really pushed the boundaries of non-objecthood. Artists like Lawrence Weiner, whose piece "An Accumulation of Information Taken from Here to There" (1969) was exhibited in the Software exhibition catalogue

or Yoko Ono whose "instrution pieces" were often impossilbe to "exectue", such that they could only ever exist as an idea.
